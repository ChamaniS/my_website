Reinforcement Learning Based Control of Cancer Chemotherapy Treatments

Project Description: Reinforcement learning has achieved tremendous success in recent years. Extending its scope from the traditional complex games, reinforcement learning has caught researcherâ€™s attention on modeling real life scenarios such as self-driving vehicles technology, industry automation, trading and finance, natural language processing, healthcare, engineering, robotics, etc. A dynamic treatment regimen, an essential core for applying personalized medicine, is one such preliminary research field in the healthcare domain, which researchers are focusing their mindsets currently. Estimating the optimal dynamic treatment regimens from the observational real clinical data is being popularized among the researchers widening its scope towards more advanced versions of reinforcement learning algorithms. This research is about a research study that focused on reinforcement learning techniques that have been approached in the context of cancer chemotherapy treatments. Primarily, this thesis addresses the result of the study by means of the state-of-the-art on reinforcement learning techniques used in generating the optimal policy for sequential treatments including the existing online reinforcement learning approaches. A simulation for cancer chemotherapy via traditional Q-learning would be thereby presented. Secondly, the study will extend towards using the offline reinforcement learning in the optimal policy generation. The proposed solution follows thus a data driven approach which works on the real human colon cancer clinical data. The novelty of the proposed solution resides on the implementation of an offline reinforcement learning approach by means of a model-based architecture using the conservative Q-learning and the model based offline policy optimization algorithm. As per the experimental results and the evaluations, it was revealed that the proposed Chemo-RL model caters well the problem of cancer chemotherapy treatment scheduling as a supportive model for the junior oncologists in deciding their treatment decisions. link.
